# Task Analysis — Outreach and Interview Guide

**Purpose:** Understand what learning engineers actually do in practice — the tasks, tools, decisions, and knowledge that define the role — to inform the learning engineering resource corpus and identify gaps.

**Target participants:** People identified in `titlesearch/data/people.json` with `triage: CANDIDATE` or `triage: APPROVED`, priority on diverse sectors (higher ed, industry, defense, international).

---

## Sample outreach email

> **Subject:** 20-min conversation about your work as a learning engineer?

Hi [First Name],

I came across your profile while building a research-backed resource library for the learning engineering community. Your work at [Organization] caught my attention, and I'd love to ask you a few questions about your day-to-day practice.

I'm conducting brief (20–30 minute) task analysis interviews to understand what learning engineers actually do — the decisions you make, the tools you rely on, and what makes the work hard. The goal is to identify where the field lacks good resources, not to evaluate anyone's approach.

A few things to know:
- The conversation is informal — no preparation needed
- I'll take notes but won't quote you by name without permission
- You can stop at any point or skip any question
- If useful to you, I'm happy to share a summary of what I hear across participants

Would you be open to a short call? I'm flexible on timing — you can grab a slot here: **[Calendly/scheduling link]**, or just reply and we'll find a time.

Thanks for considering it, and either way — thanks for the work you do.

[Your name]  
[Title], [Organization]  
[Email] | [LinkedIn URL]

## Task analysis interview guide

### Opening (2 min)

Explain the purpose again briefly. Confirm consent to take notes. Ask if they have questions before starting.

---

### Section 1 — Role and context (5 min)

1. Can you describe your current role in your own words? What do you actually *do* day to day?
2. How did you end up with the title "learning engineer" — did you choose it, or was it given to you?
3. Is there a working definition of learning engineering that resonates with you, or do you have your own?

> *Probe if needed:* How would you explain your role to a family member? How does it differ from "instructional designer"?

---

### Section 2 — Core tasks (10 min)

4. Walk me through a recent project — from the moment you got involved to the point it was "done." What were the key steps?
5. What decisions did you have to make that required the most thought or expertise?
6. What does a typical week look like? What activities take up most of your time?
7. What does it look like when you apply learning science or data to a design decision? Can you give a concrete example?
8. What gets skipped or deprioritized when you're under time or resource pressure? What does that tell you about what's actually core to the role?

> *Probe if needed:* Where does the evidence come from? How do you know if something worked?

---

### Section 3 — Tools, methods, and knowledge (5 min)

9. What tools do you use regularly — authoring tools, data tools, collaboration tools, anything?
10. What methods or frameworks do you actually apply (vs. methods you learned about but rarely use)?
11. What subject-matter knowledge matters most in your day-to-day work — learning science, data analysis, UX, something else?
12. What's a knowledge or skill gap you've felt in your own practice? What would have helped?

---

### Section 4 — Collaboration and environment (3 min)

13. Who do you work most closely with — what roles, and how do those relationships work?
14. Where does learning engineering sit in your organization — under HR, academic affairs, product, somewhere else? Does that placement affect what you can do?
15. How does your organization measure or value what you do? Is that the right measure?

---

### Section 5 — Challenges and the field (3 min)

16. What's the hardest part of your job that you don't think people outside the field understand?
17. What do you think the learning engineering field is missing right now — in terms of tools, research, community, or training?
18. Are there resources — papers, communities, tools, people — that you'd point a new learning engineer toward?

---

### Closing (2 min)

19. Is there anything about your work that I didn't ask about, that you think would be important to know?
20. Would you be willing to be contacted for a follow-up if questions come up? And is there someone else you'd recommend I talk to?

Thank them. Confirm note-sharing preference. Ask if they'd like a copy of the summary findings.

---

## Analysis notes

After each interview, capture:
- **Top 3 tasks** the participant described as core
- **Tools named**
- **Knowledge gaps** mentioned
- **Sector / context** (affects what's generalizable)
- **Surprising or unexpected finding**
- **Follow-on contact** suggested

Cross-interview patterns will feed directly into the corpus gap analysis in `data/gap_tracker.json`.
