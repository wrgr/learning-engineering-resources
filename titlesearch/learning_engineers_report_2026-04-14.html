Executive Summary

"Learning Engineer" is an emerging, contested professional title rooted in Carnegie Mellon's Open Learning Initiative and the ICICLE consortium. This survey enumerates individuals who hold it explicitly — not instructional designers who happen to use the phrase. Of 211 records, 81 (38%) are confirmed title-holders; another 102 (48%) are candidates pending profile verification.

Core finding: The title is real, used globally, and growing — but means different things in different sectors. CMU METALS graduates form the most cohesive definitional community. Independent uses in EdTech, defense, healthcare, and corporate L&D vary significantly in scope. France is a surprising #2 by country (8%), driven by corporate L&D adoption of the English term.
Why Discovery Is Hard
Title fragmentation. Practitioners doing learning-engineering work use older labels (instructional designer, curriculum engineer, learning architect) — those won't surface in a title-keyword search.
ML contamination. "Machine / Deep / Robot / Reinforcement Learning Engineer" share the trigram. Auto-triage flags ~7–8% of LinkedIn hits as ML false-positives.
LinkedIn gatekeeping. API is not public; only PhantomBuster-style scrapes return structured data, and only headlines — full profiles require manual review.
Web snippet truncation. Brave API snippets are 150–200 chars and rarely reproduce LinkedIn headlines verbatim. Only 4 confirmed records came from 131 company searches.

Section 03 · Proposed Task Analysis

From Registry to KSA Framework

This registry is the foundation for a multi-method Cognitive Task Analysis (CTA) study to characterize what Learning Engineers actually do — the KSAs that define the role across sectors, and how they differ from instructional designers, ML engineers, and ed researchers.

Phase 1
Stratified Sampling

Draw 15–20 confirmed practitioners from APPROVED, stratified by sector (EdTech, defense, higher ed, corporate) and career stage. Anchor on CMU METALS graduates; add independents for breadth.

Phase 2A
Critical Incident Technique

Semi-structured 45–60 min interviews probing three episodes: success, challenge, ambiguous case. Extract task sequences, tools, decision triggers, tacit knowledge.

Phase 2B
CTA Knowledge Probing

Knowledge Audit + Concept Mapping. Elicit participants' mental model of LE work; distinguish from adjacent titles; identify where they get stuck.

Phase 3
Card Sort — KSA Taxonomy

Hybrid open+closed sort of 80–120 candidate KSA items from interview analysis. Reveals shared structure and cross-sector divergence.

Phase 4
Survey Validation

Quantitative survey to full APPROVED + CANDIDATE pool (183 records). Validates KSA clusters; measures importance × frequency of task categories.

Phase 5
KSA Synthesis

Integrate interview, card sort, and survey data into a formal KSA framework. Publish as reference for hiring rubrics, curriculum, certification (ICICLE / ISPI).

Open Research Questions

Q1 · Is there a coherent LE role?
Do CMU METALS grads, USAF LEs, and French "Digital Learning Engineers" share a meaningful KSA core — or are these three distinct professions using the same label?
Q2 · LE vs. Instructional Designer?
Many CANDIDATEs carry "Instructional Designer" as current title. Where do practitioners draw the line — and is the distinction perceived as meaningful?
Q3 · Is France a second epicenter?
17 French practitioners (8%) is disproportionate to France's share of global EdTech employment. Are there French-language training programs driving adoption of the English title?
Q4 · METALS pipeline rate?
If ~15–20% of METALS graduates take LE titles, it is the single most productive pipeline. How does this compare to MIT ODL, ASU LEI, Georgia Tech?
