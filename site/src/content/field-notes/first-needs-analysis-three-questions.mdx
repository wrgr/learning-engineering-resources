When someone brings a learning engineer a project, they usually arrive with a solution in hand. "We need training on the new compliance policy." "We need onboarding for the analytics platform." "We need a course on data literacy."

Those are hypotheses, not needs. If you design directly from the hypothesis, you will build exactly what was asked for and exactly the wrong thing. The cost of realizing that mistake grows fast — learners sit through the course, your team ships the deliverable, and only months later do you discover the metric you were hired to move didn't budge.

The intake is your one cheap chance to reframe. Three questions, in this order, almost always get you to the real brief.

## 1. "What does success look like six months from now?"

You're not asking for the course objective. You're asking what's true about the world when this intervention has worked. Listen for *observable change* — not satisfaction scores, not completion rates, but something someone would actually be able to point to.

Good answers sound like: *"Our call center agents resolve billing disputes in one call instead of escalating them."* Or: *"New hires on the platform team ship their first pull request in week three instead of week eight."*

Bad answers sound like: *"Learners have mastered the material."* That's a restatement of the same hypothesis you started with. Push back gently: "and if they have, what changes?"

If your sponsor can't name a concrete observable change, the project isn't ready to scope. You need a different conversation first.

## 2. "What have you already tried, and what happened?"

Almost every "we need training" request is a second or third attempt at the same problem. People tried a job aid. They tried a slack reminder. They tried shadowing. You want to know what was tried, when, for how long, and why it didn't stick.

Two things to listen for:

- **What got tried that you'd actually want to reuse.** If there's a decent job aid sitting in a Confluence page, your course probably shouldn't duplicate it — it should direct learners *to* it at the right moment.
- **What got tried and produced silent failure.** If someone says "we rolled out training last year," the next question is "how did you know it worked?" Usually the answer is a smoke-screen metric (completion rate, survey score). You now know what to *not* repeat as your own evidence plan.

## 3. "Who is the skeptic?"

Every project has one. The ops VP who doesn't think the team has a learning problem, thinks they have a staffing problem. The engineer who thinks the bug rate is about tooling, not training. The clinician who thinks the new protocol is the real issue, not how people were taught.

You want to know who the skeptic is because three things follow from it:

1. **Their objection is usually half right.** If the ops VP thinks it's a staffing problem, the training you design probably needs to reduce the staffing load, not just teach the current staff better.
2. **Your evidence plan has to convince them, not the sponsor.** Sponsors are already convinced; skeptics are where the work lives.
3. **Bringing them into the design early is almost always cheaper than trying to persuade them afterward.**

## A note on what *not* to do in the intake

Don't commit to a format. Don't commit to a modality. Don't commit to a duration. The sponsor will push for it because they're trying to plan calendars and budgets. You can give a range ("somewhere between a two-hour workshop and a six-week program — I'll know in a week"), but not a number. Committing to format before you've answered these three questions is how you end up building an eLearning module for a problem that needed a checklist.
