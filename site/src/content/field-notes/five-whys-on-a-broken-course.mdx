End-of-term surveys ask learners what they thought. Five Whys asks why a specific outcome happened. The difference matters: surveys get you opinions on the experience, Five Whys gets you a causal chain you can intervene in.

The protocol is almost embarrassingly simple. You start with an observed failure — not a complaint, a failure — and ask "why?" five times, following the most load-bearing cause each time. Root Cause Analysis literature calls this the "single-thread" rule: don't branch into every possible cause, follow the one that, if fixed, would most likely have prevented the failure.

## A worked example

Observation: the final project pass rate on a six-week data analysis course dropped from 78% last cohort to 54% this cohort. Instructor is the same. Materials are the same. Rubric is the same.

1. **Why did the pass rate drop?** Learners submitted projects that missed the "justify your model choice" rubric item.
2. **Why did they miss that rubric item?** The prompt asked them to "choose a model appropriate for the data" — justification wasn't called out as a required deliverable.
3. **Why wasn't justification called out?** Last cohort's prompt template had a "justification" section header; this cohort's didn't.
4. **Why did the template change?** Someone simplified the prompt between cohorts during a "student UX" pass.
5. **Why did a student-UX simplification drop a rubric-critical field?** The simplification reviewer didn't have access to the rubric — they were reviewing for clarity, not completeness.

The fix is not "tell learners to justify their models." The fix is "rubric review of any prompt change." Different intervention, different cost, different place to add a check.

A survey would have told you "the prompt was confusing." Five Whys told you the review process had a hole.

## How to run it without it becoming a ritual

A few rules I've landed on:

- **Do it on one failure at a time.** If you find yourself running Five Whys on "engagement is down," you're too abstract. Pick a specific assignment, a specific cohort, a specific drop.
- **Write the chain down as you go.** If you can't write "Because X, therefore Y" for each step, the step is a guess, not a cause.
- **Stop at the systemic cause, not the blame-able one.** "Because the instructor forgot" is almost never the real root cause. The real cause is usually "because there was no structural reason the instructor *would* have remembered."
- **End with a check, not just a fix.** For every root cause you identify, ask: "what check would have caught this before it hit learners?" Add that check.

## When not to use it

Five Whys is for single-thread causal failures. It's bad for multi-cause phenomena ("learners disengage") and for anything involving external drift (hiring-market changes, new prerequisite gaps). For those, you want something with branching — Fishbone is the usual next step.
