A Bibliometrically-Grounded Evidence Workspace for Learning Engineering: Purpose, Pedagogical Foundations, and Design Novelty

Produced from the Learning Engineering Resources corpus pipeline  ·  v1.0, April 2026  ·  IEEE ICICLE

Abstract. Learning engineering is a nascent, interdisciplinary practice that applies the science of learning to the design of effective educational products and experiences. Its literature is scattered across cognitive science, educational psychology, instructional design, human-computer interaction, and AI-in-education — with no single curated, navigable entry point. This whitepaper describes the purpose, pedagogical foundations, and design novelty of the Learning Engineering Resources evidence workspace: a topic-linked, bibliometrically-grounded site that maps the field's canonical and emerging papers to 18 core topics, co-locates non-paper resources alongside academic literature, and renders the underlying citation network as a live, explorable graph. The corpus is constructed via a principled pipeline — seed curation, one-hop OpenAlex expansion, cross-seed connectivity filtering, and k-core/in-degree graph pruning — that produces an auditable, updatable evidence base rather than a static bibliography. The workspace surfaces provenance at every node, enabling practitioners, researchers, and curriculum designers to trace not just what the field says, but why a given paper or resource earned its place.

1. Purpose: Why This Workspace Exists

Practitioners entering or deepening their engagement with learning engineering face a practical problem: the evidence base is large, fragmented, and inconsistently labeled. A practitioner asking "what does the research say about adaptive learning systems?" must navigate across cognitive science journals, instructional design conference proceedings, edtech preprints, government policy reports, and practitioner white papers — with no systematic guide to what is foundational, what is emerging, and what connects to what.

The standard alternatives are insufficient. A curated reading list captures expert judgment but lacks scale, provenance, and updateability. A raw search query on Google Scholar or OpenAlex returns an unfiltered flood with no topical structure. A literature review article synthesizes a moment in time but cannot be re-run, filtered, or navigated interactively.

This workspace is designed as a third option: a living, topic-linked evidence base that is simultaneously algorithmically grounded (selection criteria are explicit and reproducible) and humanly curated (seeds are chosen by domain experts; every inclusion decision can be traced). It is targeted at three audiences:

  • Practitioners who want to ground their design decisions in evidence and need a navigable, trustworthy starting point.
  • Researchers who want to understand how the field's canonical literature is structured, which topics are well-covered, and where gaps exist.
  • Curriculum designers and program leaders who need to map evidence to specific competency areas and identify gaps in their own course or program coverage.

The site is not a replacement for engagement with the primary literature. It is an orientation layer — a structured map of a complex territory that helps users know where to look and why what they find there matters.

2. Learning Engineering as a Discipline

Learning engineering has been defined as "a process and practice that applies the learning sciences and human-centered engineering design methodology and process to support learners and their development" (Goodell & Kolodner, 2020). The field sits at the intersection of learning science, educational technology, and human-centered design — distinct from each of its constituent disciplines in its emphasis on iterative, data-informed improvement of learning experiences at scale.

Several features of learning engineering as a field create distinctive challenges for evidence navigation:

Disciplinary fragmentation

Learning engineering draws from at least six parent disciplines — cognitive psychology, educational psychology, instructional systems design, computer science, human-computer interaction, and measurement science — each with its own journals, citation norms, and methodological traditions. A single practitioner question (e.g., "how should I design feedback?") may have partial answers in cognitive load theory (Sweller, 1988), formative assessment research (Black & Wiliam, 1998), ITS feedback design (VanLehn, 2011), and reinforcement learning in tutoring systems (Chi et al., 2011) — papers that rarely cite each other.

Rapidly evolving frontier

The integration of AI into learning systems has accelerated dramatically since 2020. Large language models, generative tutoring agents, and AI-powered assessment are producing a high-velocity stream of preprints and conference papers that has not yet been consolidated into textbooks or systematic reviews. A static bibliography becomes outdated within months; an updatable pipeline does not.

Non-paper resources are first-class artifacts

Much of what practitioners actually use — degree programs, professional certifications, practitioner communities, software tools, and policy frameworks — does not appear in citation databases at all. Any evidence workspace that covers only the academic literature systematically excludes the resources practitioners most frequently access.

The corpus design decisions documented in this whitepaper are direct responses to these three challenges.

3. Pedagogical Foundations

The workspace is itself designed according to principles from the learning sciences — the field it documents. This is intentional: a workspace for learning engineering practitioners should embody what it describes.

3.1 Cognitive load and chunking

Cognitive load theory (Sweller, 1988; Sweller et al., 1998) holds that working memory has a finite capacity, and that learning materials should minimize extraneous load while maximizing germane load. A flat list of 295 papers imposes high extraneous load — it forces the user to impose structure that the workspace has already computed. Topic-based clustering (18 topics, each with its own paper group and resource set) reduces extraneous load by presenting the field's existing conceptual structure rather than an unordered flood.

This is directly analogous to how learning engineering textbooks and curricula are organized: not alphabetically or chronologically, but around functional knowledge areas (assessment, instructional design, feedback, engagement, etc.) that map to genuine use cases.

3.2 Spaced retrieval and multiple entry points

Research on retrieval practice (Roediger & Karpicke, 2006; Kornell & Bjork, 2008) demonstrates that encountering material in multiple contexts strengthens retention and transfer. The workspace provides three independent navigation paths to the same underlying corpus: the citation graph (structural/relational entry), the topic-based resource navigator (categorical entry), and the search interface (query-driven entry). A practitioner who encounters a paper via the graph and then finds it again in the topic navigator is having a spaced retrieval experience that deepens familiarity with the evidence base.

3.3 Metacognitive transparency (provenance at every node)

Metacognitive research demonstrates that learners who understand the structure of a domain learn it more effectively (Flavell, 1979; Zimmerman, 2002). This workspace exposes its own construction logic at every point: each paper carries its selection tier, cross-seed score, and topic assignments; each node in the graph displays its provenance JSON; each topic group shows its count and gap status. Users are not just reading a curated list — they are reading a curated list that tells them why it was curated this way.

This is analogous to explicit modeling in direct instruction: the expert doesn't just demonstrate the answer, they make their reasoning transparent. A practitioner who understands why a paper is in the corpus (it was cited by three or more seeds, passes the k-core filter, and is assigned to Adaptive Systems and Assessment) understands something about the field's structure, not just about the paper.

3.4 Scaffolded exploration (seed → expansion → signal)

Zone of proximal development theory (Vygotsky, 1978) and related work on scaffolding (Wood et al., 1976) suggest that effective learning environments provide support structures that can be progressively removed as competence increases. The corpus's three-tier structure — core papers (21 seed papers representing canonical anchors), related papers (274 algorithmically expanded papers), and coverage signals (gap analysis) — provides a natural scaffold. A novice can start with core papers alone; an expert can explore the full expansion and use the coverage signals to identify where the field's evidence is thin.

The toggle to "include related papers" in the graph workspace makes this scaffolding explicit and user-controlled: learners choose when to expand their view.

3.5 Worked examples and visible reasoning

Worked example research (Sweller & Cooper, 1985; Atkinson et al., 2000) shows that seeing complete, annotated solutions is more effective for novices than problem-solving from scratch. The citation graph is a structural worked example: it shows how the field's ideas connect, how seeds relate to expansion papers, and which topics serve as bridges between subfields. A practitioner learning to do a literature review in learning engineering can use the graph as a reference model for what a well-structured evidence map looks like.

4. Corpus Construction: The Pipeline

The corpus is constructed through a reproducible, eight-step pipeline grounded in bibliometrics and graph theory. The full methodology is documented separately in the Corpus Construction Methodology; this section summarizes the design choices and their rationale.

4.1 Seed selection as the primary quality gate

Twenty-one seed papers were manually selected by domain experts to represent the field's canonical topics. Seed selection criteria include: explicit topic coverage (every major topic must have at least two seeds), diversity of document type (landmark studies, systematic reviews, consensus frameworks, and advocacy documents), author and institutional diversity, and recency balance (foundational anchors alongside papers representing the current frontier).

Seeds are the primary quality gate for the entire pipeline. Algorithmic sophistication downstream cannot compensate for a poor seed set. This is why seeds are chosen by human experts, not by an algorithm.

4.2 One-hop expansion via OpenAlex

From each seed, the pipeline retrieves both backward citations (papers the seed cites) and forward citations (papers that cite the seed) using the OpenAlex API (Priem et al., 2022). This generates a raw candidate pool of several thousand papers — everything that the field's canonical literature is directly connected to in the global citation graph.

Both directions are necessary. Backward citations capture the papers a seed treats as foundational — its intellectual predecessors. Forward citations capture the papers the field has recognized as building on the seed — its intellectual descendants. A corpus that uses only backward citations systematically excludes recent and emerging work; one that uses only forward citations misses foundational predecessors.

4.3 Cross-seed connectivity filter

The key structural filter is the cross-seed score: the count of seeds (not review anchors) in whose one-hop neighborhood a candidate paper appears. Papers with cross-seed score ≥ 2 pass the primary filter.

cross_seed_score(paper) = |{seed ∈ seeds : paper ∈ neighbors(seed)}|

A paper passing this filter has been recognized by the field's own citation behavior as relevant to multiple canonical anchors — independently of any editorial decision.

This is the most principled selection criterion available: it grounds inclusion in the collective behavior of the scientific community rather than in any single metric, institutional affiliation, or editorial choice. A paper from a small institution with modest citation count that is cited by or cites five seeds has demonstrated structural importance in the field.

4.4 k-core and in-degree graph pruning

After the cross-seed filter, the surviving candidate set is treated as a citation subgraph. Two additional graph filters are applied:

  • 2-core constraint: Only nodes that belong to a 2-core of the citation subgraph are retained. A 2-core is the maximal subgraph in which every node has at least two neighbors — it removes peripheral papers that connect to the core through a single edge. This ensures the final corpus has the dense, mutually-reinforcing connectivity of a coherent literature rather than the fragmented structure of a loosely related collection.
  • In-degree ≥ 2 constraint: Each retained paper must be cited by at least two other papers in the corpus. This excludes papers that cite field-canonical work but are not themselves cited within the field — i.e., papers at the fringe of relevance.

Together these two filters produce a citation graph with 339 nodes and 1,261 edges — a highly connected, internally coherent subgraph representing the field's structural core.

4.5 Non-paper resources as first-class artifacts

The corpus includes 26 non-paper resources — degree programs, practitioner organizations, professional communities, software platforms, and policy frameworks — co-located with papers under the same topic structure. These are drawn from a curated registry maintained alongside the academic corpus. Including them is a deliberate design choice: learning engineering is a practice field, and the resources practitioners use to do the practice are part of the evidence base for the practice.

5. Design Novelty

Several features of this workspace are, to our knowledge, novel or unusually complete for a learning engineering evidence resource:

5.1 Bibliometrically grounded, not editorially curated

Most curated learning engineering reading lists are the product of a single expert's or committee's judgment, with inclusion criteria that are implicit and non-reproducible. This corpus uses explicit, computable selection criteria (cross-seed score ≥ 2; 2-core + in-degree ≥ 2) that can be re-run against a new or updated seed set, producing a new corpus edition with the same structural guarantees. This makes the corpus auditable — any inclusion can be traced back to the criteria that selected it — and updatable without starting from scratch.

5.2 Provenance at every node

Every paper in the workspace carries its full provenance: selection tier, cross-seed score, topic assignments, citation count, and the specific seeds that contributed to its inclusion. This is surfaced in the UI — selecting any graph node reveals its provenance JSON. This is unusual: most literature tools show you the paper; this one shows you the paper and the argument for why it is in the set.

5.3 Live citation graph as navigation

The citation graph is not a static image. It is an interactive force-directed network in which every node is clickable, displays its type (topic, paper, resource), and reveals its citation context (what it cites, what cites it). This makes the structure of the field's literature a navigable artifact, not just a description of one. A user can literally follow citation paths through the graph, discovering how ideas connect across topics.

5.4 Topic-paper-resource triangulation

Each of the 18 topics in the workspace is linked to seed papers, expansion papers, and non-paper resources. This triangulation — "for Topic X, here is the academic evidence, here is the emerging literature, and here are the programs and tools practitioners use" — is, to our knowledge, not available in any other single interface for learning engineering.

5.5 Coverage signal layer

The workspace includes a coverage signals section that reports, in real time from the current corpus state, which topics have no non-paper resources, how many papers lack abstract text, and what known gaps exist in the evidence base. This self-auditing layer is rare in curated evidence resources. It makes the workspace's limitations visible rather than hiding them, and creates an explicit mechanism for the workspace maintainers and community to prioritize additions.

5.6 Fully static, zero-backend architecture

The workspace is a static site: all data is pre-computed into JSON files, and the browser renders the entire experience with no server-side calls at runtime. This means it is deployable as a GitHub Pages site at zero marginal cost, is trivially forkable and adaptable to other domains, and is resilient to API rate limits and backend failures. The Python corpus pipeline runs at build time; the UI consumes its outputs. This separation makes the workspace both durable and extensible.

6. Limitations and Future Directions

6.1 Known corpus limitations

The corpus construction methodology has documented systematic biases that users should understand:

  • English-language bias. OpenAlex has stronger coverage of English-language literature. Non-English publications, including substantial bodies of learning engineering research from Germany, Japan, and Latin America, are systematically underrepresented.
  • Institutional connectivity bias. Papers from well-connected institutions (large R1 universities, well-funded labs) accumulate cross-seed connectivity faster than equivalent papers from peripheral or emerging institutions. The corpus is not immune to prestige effects.
  • Grey literature gap. Conference abstracts, practitioner reports, government white papers, and institutional self-studies are not well-indexed by citation APIs. The non-paper resource registry partially compensates, but coverage is incomplete.
  • Recency-velocity inflation. Very recent papers can show high citation velocity that decays as the field normalizes around them. Emerging papers should be interpreted with awareness of this effect.
  • Seed subjectivity. While the expansion and filtering steps are algorithmic, the seed set remains a human judgment. A different expert might choose different seeds, producing a different corpus. The current seed set is documented in the corpus registry for transparency.

6.2 Future directions

Several extensions are planned or under consideration:

  • Retrieval-augmented generation (RAG) layer. The corpus is structured for downstream RAG integration. A conversational interface that lets practitioners ask natural language questions against the evidence base, with citations surfaced to the specific papers and sections that ground each answer, would substantially lower the access barrier for practitioners who are not comfortable navigating a citation graph.
  • Velocity-tier emerging literature. The current corpus uses seed connectivity as the primary filter. A second pass using citation velocity (citations per year) to surface high-momentum recent papers not yet well-connected to seeds would extend coverage of the emerging frontier.
  • Multi-language coverage. Explicit Tier 4 allocations for non-English publications — following the methodology's recommendation for domains with international research communities — would reduce the English-language bias.
  • Community contribution mechanism. An open pull request process for practitioners and researchers to propose additions to the non-paper resource registry or flag gaps would allow the workspace to benefit from the expertise of the broader learning engineering community.

7. Conclusion

The Learning Engineering Resources evidence workspace is designed to do something specific: give practitioners, researchers, and curriculum designers a principled, navigable, and honest map of a complex and scattered literature. It does this by combining the rigor of bibliometric corpus construction with the navigability of an interactive citation graph and the practical breadth of a curated non-paper resource set.

The workspace is honest about its limitations — in the coverage signals section, in the provenance JSON attached to every node, and in this document. An evidence resource that hides its gaps is less useful than one that makes them visible. The goal is not to produce the appearance of comprehensive coverage, but to produce a trustworthy, auditable starting point that helps users understand both what the field knows and how well we know it.

Learning engineering is a field that exists to improve learning at scale. A workspace that makes its evidence base more accessible, navigable, and trustworthy is a small contribution to that larger project.
